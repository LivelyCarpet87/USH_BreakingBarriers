Before the iPhone 2G

Before the iPhone, phones were portable but limited to calling only. Though they were able to be used for other purposes, their uses were not well recognized because of complicated interface. Most phones used simplified keypads that took up space. The small screens and complicated controls made it difficult to use.

Limitations

  1. Interface:

    For example, the Nokia E63 could only browse the web with its arrow keys. This limitation made browsing slow and it was hard to control the mouse. All of its features, ranging from unit conversion to office documents, were hard to reach behind multiple layers of menus. Its smaller screen size limited its ability to display interfaces and its keyboard-only controls were difficult to learn. (Nokia E63 User Guide)

  2. Memory Size:

    The older phones before the iPhone had extremely limited storage. For example, the Nokia E63 had 110 Mb of user storage (Cha). The total user memory size is equivalent to about 61 photos taken with a modern 13 MP phone camera (Ken W. Watson). This limitation prevented the phone from taking on simple tasks possible on a computer because they needed more memory. Games, offline maps, social media, music/video streaming were not feasible because of this limitation.

"I had a flip phone until I was 25, and I didn't use social media until that age, either."

--Haley Joel Osment (Riesman)

How Limitations Formed the Barrier

These limitations prevented the phones from taking on tasks we are habituated to today without highly complex workarounds. Examples of workarounds were to limited features, online content, text-based interfaces. These workarounds and features were hard to comprehend and required detailed understanding of the manuals to discover and comprehend. The difficulty associated with the use of advanced features effectively became the barrier of technical complexity stopping users from using mobile phones to their full potential.
